Explore General Geometry Concepts

This tab gives students a quick preview of ideas they will see in Geometry. It is for review and confidence-building, not scoring.

1. Points, Lines, and Angles

Geometry begins with naming and describing shapes accurately.

  • A point is an exact location.
  • A line continues forever in both directions.
  • An angle is formed by two rays with the same endpoint.
Example: A straight angle measures 180°. A right angle measures 90°.

2. Angle Relationships

Many Geometry problems are solved by recognizing how angles are related.

  • Complementary angles add to 90°.
  • Supplementary angles add to 180°.
  • Vertical angles are across from each other and are equal.
Example: If two supplementary angles include 120°, the other angle is 60°.

3. Triangles

Triangles show up constantly because they are simple but powerful shapes.

  • The three angles in a triangle add to 180°.
  • Triangles can be classified by sides or angles.
  • Congruent triangles have the same size and shape.
Example: If two triangle angles are 50° and 70°, the third angle is 60°.

4. Area, Perimeter, and Volume

Measurement depends on knowing what each measure means.

  • Perimeter is distance around a shape.
  • Area is the surface inside a flat shape.
  • Volume is the space inside a 3D object.
Example: A 5 by 8 rectangle has area 40 square units and perimeter 26 units.

5. Coordinate Geometry

Geometry often connects shapes to the coordinate plane.

  • Points are written as ordered pairs, like (3, -2).
  • Slope describes steepness.
  • Distance and midpoint can be found using coordinates.
Example: The point (-4, 2) is in Quadrant II.

6. Transformations

Transformations describe how figures move or change.

  • A translation slides a figure.
  • A reflection flips a figure.
  • A rotation turns a figure.
  • A dilation resizes a figure.
Example: Sliding a triangle 4 units right is a translation.

7. Logic and Proofs

Geometry asks students to explain why something must be true.

  • Use facts in a clear order.
  • Support each step with a reason.
  • Look for what must be true, not just what might be true.
Example: If all squares are rectangles and this shape is a square, then it must also be a rectangle.

8. What Readiness Looks Like

A student does not need to know all Geometry before taking Geometry. They should be able to work through new information without getting completely stuck.

  • Comfort with basic algebra
  • Willingness to read diagrams carefully
  • Ability to follow multi-step reasoning
  • Basic number sense with fractions, negatives, and formulas
Tip: If these ideas feel unfamiliar but understandable, the screener is a good next step.
